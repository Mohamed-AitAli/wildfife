Why a Skoolie?

A full-size school bus gives you 250‚Äì300 square feet of living space ‚Äî more than many studio apartments. Standing room throughout. Room for a real kitchen, a full bathroom, a king bed, and still space to breathe. It's the most home you'll get for the money, but it comes at a cost: this is a serious multi-month project.

üöå Is a Skoolie Right for You? If you want the most space and are willing to put in 6‚Äì18 months of work, yes. If you want to be on the road in 3 months and hate DIY, start with a van.

Buying the Right Bus

Type C vs Type D

Type C (conventional nose, dog nose) buses have the engine in front ‚Äî easier to work on, more familiar. Type D (flat nose, transit style) have more interior space but the engine is under a panel inside. For conversion purposes, either works. Most skoolie builders use Type C International or Blue Bird buses.

What to Look For

  • Mileage matters less than maintenance ‚Äî a well-maintained 200K mile bus beats a neglected 80K one
  • Check the frame underneath for rust penetration ‚Äî surface rust is fine, structural rust is a dealbreaker
  • Inspect the floor condition ‚Äî replacing a bus floor is a major job
  • Run the AC and heat before you buy
  • Check diesel vs gas ‚Äî diesel is more expensive upfront but better for long-term economics
  • Request maintenance records ‚Äî schools keep good records

Where to Buy

  • Government auction sites ‚Äî GovPlanet, PublicSurplus (best prices)
  • Facebook Marketplace ‚Äî huge selection, negotiate hard
  • School district surplus ‚Äî contact local districts directly
  • Craigslist ‚Äî still active in many regions

The Build Timeline

Week 1‚Äì2

Demo & Assessment

Remove seats, flooring, interior panels. Assess rust situation. This reveals your real scope of work.

Week 2‚Äì4

Rust Treatment & Metalwork

Wire brush, naval jelly, rust converter, then encapsulate. Optional: raised roof cut (major project requiring a professional welder or serious skills).

Week 4‚Äì6

Subfloor & Insulation

New subfloor over treated metal. Insulate ceiling, walls, wheel wells. This is the step where skoolies differ most from vans ‚Äî more area means more time and cost.

Week 6‚Äì10

Electrical Rough-In

Run all wiring before walls close up. Solar, 12V DC, 120V AC, battery bank. Skoolies typically need 600‚Äì1000W of solar and 400Ah+ of batteries.

Week 10‚Äì16

Walls, Ceiling & Flooring

Paneling, ceiling material, finish flooring. This is where your design vision starts becoming real.

Week 16‚Äì24

Furniture & Systems

Kitchen build, bed, bathroom (if included), plumbing, HVAC. Most of your budget and time is here.

Week 24+

Finish Work & Move In

Trim, paint, soft furnishings, organization. Test every system before your first long trip.

The Raised Roof Question

A standard school bus has about 6' of interior standing height ‚Äî fine for most people. A raised roof adds 12‚Äì18 inches, bringing you to 7'+ of clearance. The cost: $2,000‚Äì$6,000 for a professional cut, or extreme DIY skill and a plasma cutter. For full-timers over 5'10", it's often worth it. For weekend use, not necessary.
